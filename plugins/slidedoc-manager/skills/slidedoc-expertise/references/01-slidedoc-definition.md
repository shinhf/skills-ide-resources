# Nancy Duarte's Slidedoc -- Definition & Core Concept

> **Source:** Nancy Duarte, CEO of Duarte Inc. Author of six best-selling books.
> Fourth book: *Slidedocs: Spread Ideas with Effective Visual Documents* (free ebook).

## What Is a Slidedoc?

A **Slidedoc** is a visual document developed in presentation software (like PowerPoint)
that is designed to be **read and referenced** rather than projected and watched.

It combines **words and images** to deliver and clarify information in a **skimmable format**,
allowing audiences to digest ideas **at their own pace**.

### Key Characteristics

- **Handed out and read**, not projected and watched
- Pairs **visuals + text** in a succinct and meaningful way
- Key messages are understood **without a presenter present**
- Built in **presentation software** (PowerPoint, Google Slides, Keynote)
- Functions as a standalone, self-explanatory document
- **Word density**: approximately **150-200 words per page** (vs. 40 words or fewer for projected slides)
- **Self-sufficient**: each page contains enough information to be understood without oral explanation
- **Interactive**: supports internal hyperlinks (navigation) and external links (sources)
- **Device-friendly**: reading experience like turning pages in a book, optimized for screen consumption
- **Skimmable**: visual hierarchy and structure allow rapid scanning before deep reading

### The Core Insight

> "Presentations are one of the most powerful tools for persuasive communication,
> but they're not always the right vehicle to deliver information."
> -- Nancy Duarte

People rarely question whether a presentation is the appropriate format for their message.
An estimated 350 presentations are delivered every second globally, yet many of these
would be more effective as Slidedocs.

### An Important Nuance: Density Is Not the Problem

> Nancy Duarte (Indezine interview): "Dense slides aren't bad."

The problem is not that slides contain too much text -- the problem is **presenting
dense slides on a projector** and reading them aloud. When a dense, information-rich
slide is distributed for independent reading, it becomes a Slidedoc and serves its
audience well. The critical decision is **how the artifact is used**, not how much
text it contains.

## The Communication Spectrum

Duarte positions Slidedocs on a spectrum between traditional documents and projected presentations:

```
Document (Word/PDF)  <-->  Slidedoc  <-->  Projected Presentation
       |                      |                      |
  Text-heavy            Visual + Text           Visual-first
  Linear reading        Skimmable               Requires presenter
  Dense detail          Balanced detail          Minimal text
  No visuals needed     Integrated visuals       Cinematic visuals
```

A Slidedoc is described as **"more than an email, but less than a presentation."**

## Why Slidedocs Work

1. **Visual processing advantage**: People process visual information faster than text alone
2. **Memory retention**: Visuals stick with audiences longer than text
3. **Self-paced consumption**: Readers can spend as much time as needed on each page
4. **No presenter dependency**: The document communicates its full message independently
5. **Asynchronous sharing**: Can be distributed via email, shared drives, etc.
