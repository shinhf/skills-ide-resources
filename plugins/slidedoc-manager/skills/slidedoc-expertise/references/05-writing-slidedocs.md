# Writing a Slidedoc -- Content & Text Guidelines

> **Source:** Nancy Duarte's *Slidedocs* ebook (Chapter: "Writing a Slidedoc")
> and Duarte blog resources.

## Core Writing Principles

### 1. Write for Skimmability

Slidedocs are designed to be **skimmed first, then read in detail**. Every page must
work at two levels:
- **Skim level**: Headlines, callouts, and key visuals tell the story at a glance
- **Deep-read level**: Body text provides supporting evidence and detail

### 2. Condense Ruthlessly

> **Signal vs. Noise**: Amplify relevant content; eliminate unnecessary elements.

- Cut every word that doesn't earn its place
- Convert long paragraphs into **bullet points or numbered lists**
- Replace descriptive text with **diagrams, charts, or icons** where possible
- Use **sentence fragments** for headlines (complete sentences are for body text)
- Target **concise takeaways**, not exhaustive explanations

### 3. One Idea Per Page

Each Slidedoc page should communicate **one main idea**. This is the single most
important writing discipline:

- The **headline/title** states the key message (not a topic label)
- All content on the page **supports that one idea**
- If you have two distinct ideas, make two pages
- This enables both skimmability and clear flow

### 4. Action-Oriented Headlines

Headlines should be **conclusions, not topics**:

| Weak (Topic Label) | Strong (Conclusion) |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Q3 Revenue | Q3 Revenue Exceeded Targets by 12% |
| Customer Feedback | Customers Rank Speed as #1 Priority |
| Market Analysis | Three Competitors Are Gaining Share |
| Project Timeline | Phase 2 Launch Moves to September |

The reader should understand the **key takeaway** from the headline alone,
without reading the body text.

### 5. Use Clear, Concise Takeaways

After presenting data or analysis, always provide:
- A **clear conclusion** ("what this means")
- **Actionable next steps** ("what we should do")
- **Concise examples** to ground abstract points

### 6. Integrate Text and Visuals

Text and visuals should work **together**, not independently:
- Don't repeat in text what a chart already shows
- Use text to **interpret** visuals ("Revenue grew 15%, driven by...")
- Label charts and diagrams directly (avoid forcing readers to cross-reference legends)
- Callout boxes can highlight the most important data point from a chart

## Text Formatting Guidelines

### Typography Hierarchy
- **Page title**: Largest, boldest -- states the main message
- **Section headers**: Medium weight -- organize supporting content
- **Body text**: Standard size -- provides detail
- **Captions/labels**: Smaller -- annotate visuals
- **Callout text**: Distinct style (box, color, size) -- highlights key findings

### Text Density Rules

**Word count targets per page:**

| Context | Words/Page | Source |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Duarte's Slidedoc characteristic | **150-200** | Nancy Duarte's blog / definition of slidedoc format |
| Duarte's template recommendation | **75-175** | Official Slidedocs template design guidelines |
| Projected presentation slides (for comparison) | **40 or fewer** | Nancy Duarte's slidedoc vs. slides distinction |

Pages below ~75 words may lack enough context to be self-sufficient (unless they are
cover pages, section dividers, or impact layouts). Pages above ~200 words risk
becoming walls of text that lose the visual advantage of the Slidedoc format.

**Additional text density guidelines:**
- Aim for **readable paragraphs** (3-5 sentences max per block)
- Use **bullet lists** for 3+ related items
- Limit body text to **what fits comfortably** with adequate whitespace
- If text is overwhelming the page, split into two pages
- Remember: a Slidedoc is not a Word document pasted into PowerPoint

## Structuring a Slidedoc Document

### Recommended Sections (adapt to context)

1. **Cover page** -- title, subtitle, author, date
2. **Table of contents** -- especially for documents over 10 pages
3. **Executive summary** -- key findings and recommendations (1-2 pages)
4. **Context/background** -- the situation and why this matters
5. **Main content sections** -- findings, analysis, options, etc.
6. **Recommendations** -- what actions to take
7. **Appendix** -- supporting data, methodology, references

### Book-Like Design Elements
Slidedoc templates incorporate book-design conventions:
- Cover, table of contents, and chapter indicators
- Consistent prose sections and page numbers
- Section dividers between major topics

## The "Signal and Noise" Principle

From Duarte's "4 Tips to Perfect Slide Design":

- **Signal** = the content that matters, the message you want to communicate
- **Noise** = anything that distracts from or competes with the signal
- Every element on the page should be **signal** or be removed
- Decorative elements are noise unless they reinforce meaning
- Background patterns, unnecessary borders, and clip art are noise
