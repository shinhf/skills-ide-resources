# Delivering a Slidedoc -- Distribution, Formats & Reading Experience

> **Source:** Nancy Duarte's *Slidedocs* ebook (Chapter 4: "Delivering a Slidedoc"),
> Duarte blog posts, and Indezine interview with Nancy Duarte.

## Why Delivery Matters

A Slidedoc that is never read fails regardless of how well it was written or designed.
Delivery encompasses **how, when, and in what format** the document reaches its audience.
Because Slidedocs are explicitly designed for asynchronous consumption -- without a
presenter -- the delivery strategy is part of the communication design.

> Nancy Duarte: Slidedocs should "spread through an organization" the way a useful
> internal document does -- forwarded, bookmarked, referenced in later discussions.

## Distribution Contexts

### Pre-Read (Before a Meeting)

The most common Slidedoc use case. Distribute the Slidedoc **before** a meeting so
attendees arrive informed and ready for discussion rather than sitting through a
read-aloud of dense content.

**Best practice:** Send the Slidedoc at least **10 minutes before** the meeting
(longer for complex material). Allocate dedicated silent reading time at the start
of the meeting if pre-reading compliance is uncertain.

### Leave-Behind (After a Presentation)

When you deliver a cinematic, visual-first presentation live, the Slidedoc serves as
the detailed companion document. It provides the context, evidence, and nuance that
minimal-text projected slides cannot carry.

### Standalone Distribution

Slidedocs sent via email, shared drives, intranets, or collaboration platforms
(SharePoint, Confluence, Teams, Slack) without any accompanying meeting or presentation.
The document must be entirely self-sufficient.

### Print Distribution

For board meetings, executive reviews, or contexts where digital access is impractical.
Ensure the Slidedoc is designed to print well (adequate margins, readable font sizes
at print resolution, no critical content in bleed areas).

## Converting a Presentation to a Slidedoc Leave-Behind

When you need both a projected presentation and a leave-behind, Duarte's guidance is
clear: **create two separate decks**. Do not try to use the same slides for both.

### The Notes View Method

PowerPoint's Notes View provides a practical workflow for converting a cinematic
presentation into a Slidedoc leave-behind:

1. **Start with the cinematic presentation** (visual-first, minimal text)
2. **Switch to Notes View** in PowerPoint
3. **Add context** below each slide image: the narrative, data, evidence, and
   conclusions that the presenter would normally speak aloud

### Three Formatting Options for Leave-Behinds

| Option | Layout | Best For |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Default template** | Slide image on top + context text below (appendix style) | Quick conversion; audiences familiar with notes-page format |
| **Adjusted template** | Cinematic visual + callout visual + supplemental text side by side | Richer visual experience; when the slide visual alone needs annotation |
| **Landscape layout** | Varied typesetting to call attention to different elements across the page | Complex content requiring flexible layout; section-by-section narratives |

## Device-Friendly Reading Experience

Nancy Duarte identifies "device-friendly" as a core Slidedoc characteristic. A Slidedoc
should feel like **turning pages in a book**, not scrolling through a document.

### Why Presentation Software Works for Reading

- **Widescreen aspect ratio** (16:9) matches modern screens better than portrait documents
- **Page-by-page navigation** creates a natural reading rhythm
- Readers can **swipe or click through** at their own pace on any device
- **Full-screen reading mode** eliminates distractions (no toolbars, no other windows)
- Presentation software renders consistently across devices (unlike complex Word layouts)

### Design for Multiple Devices

- Test readability on **laptop, tablet, and phone** screens
- Ensure body text is large enough to read without zooming (minimum ~10-11pt equivalent)
- Avoid layouts that require horizontal scrolling on narrow screens
- Keep critical content within safe areas (away from edges that may be clipped)

## Export Formats

| Format | Use Case | Advantages | Limitations |
|--------|----------|------------|-------------|
| **PPTX** | Editable sharing, collaboration | Preserves editability; supports animations/links | Requires PowerPoint or compatible software |
| **PDF** | Final distribution, print, archive | Stable rendering everywhere; good for email | Not editable; hyperlink behavior varies by reader |
| **Google Slides link** | Cloud-native collaboration | Real-time access; no file management | Requires Google account; formatting may shift |
| **Printed handout** | Board meetings, in-room reading | No technology dependency | Cannot follow hyperlinks; no animation |

## Delivery Checklist

Before distributing a Slidedoc, verify:

- [ ] The document is **self-sufficient** (understandable without a presenter)
- [ ] All **hyperlinks work** (internal navigation and external sources)
- [ ] **Page numbers** are present and correct
- [ ] The **table of contents** links to the correct sections
- [ ] Fonts render correctly on the target platform (or are embedded)
- [ ] The document is **accessible** (see document 11 for accessibility checklist)
- [ ] The **export format** matches the distribution context (PPTX for collaboration, PDF for final distribution)
- [ ] The **file size** is manageable for the distribution channel (compress images if needed)
- [ ] **Metadata** is appropriate (author, date, confidentiality level)
