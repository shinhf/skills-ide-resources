# The Five Essential Design Principles (Slide:ology Framework)

> **Source:** Nancy Duarte's *Slide:ology* and Duarte's critique language framework.
> These principles apply to both projected slides and Slidedocs.

## Overview

Every slide or Slidedoc page should be evaluated against five essential design elements.
Together, they determine whether a visual communication piece is effective.

---

## 1. Contrast

**What it is:** Differences in color, size, weight, shape, or position between design elements.

**What it does:** Creates focus, drama, and visual hierarchy. Helps the reader instantly
understand what is most important on the page.

**Evaluation question:** *Is the prioritization clear, or is it indistinct?*

### Best Practices for Slidedocs
- Use **bold or larger type** for key takeaways vs. supporting details
- Apply **bright/saturated colors** to highlight critical data points
- Use **neutral/gray tones** for supporting elements (gridlines, labels, scales)
- Create visual distinction between headings, subheadings, body text, and captions
- Ensure the single most important message on each page is visually dominant

---

## 2. Whitespace

**What it is:** The empty/unused space on a page. Duarte calls it "the oxygen of a slide."

**What it does:** Drives focus, provides breathing room, and allows all other design
elements to function properly. Reduces cognitive load.

**Evaluation question:** *Is the space open, or cluttered?*

### Best Practices for Slidedocs
- Maintain generous **margins** on all sides
- Don't fill every available area with content
- Use whitespace to **group related elements** and **separate unrelated ones**
- Allow space between text blocks, charts, and images
- When in doubt, **remove** elements rather than add them
- A Slidedoc page should never feel "cramped"

---

## 3. Hierarchy

**What it is:** The visual treatment of elements according to their importance.

**What it does:** Establishes parent/child relationships between ideas. Guides the reader
from the most important information to supporting details.

**Evaluation question:** *Is a parent/child relationship apparent or absent in the ideas?*

### Best Practices for Slidedocs
- **Title/headline** is the most prominent text element on each page
- Use a consistent **heading hierarchy** (H1 > H2 > H3 > body > caption)
- The key takeaway should be identifiable within seconds
- Use **size, weight, color, and position** to establish importance
- Group supporting data beneath their governing idea
- Section headers should clearly distinguish major topics

---

## 4. Unity

**What it is:** Consistency and cohesiveness across the entire document.

**What it does:** Creates a structured, professional appearance. Makes the document
feel like it comes from a single source and tells one coherent story.

**Evaluation question:** *Does the document look structured or unstructured?*

### Best Practices for Slidedocs
- Maintain **consistent look and feel** across all pages
- Use the **same image treatment** throughout (e.g., all photos in the same style)
- Keep **element placement** predictable (titles, page numbers, logos in same position)
- Apply a **consistent color palette** (limit to 3-5 colors)
- Use the **same typefaces** throughout (typically 1-2 font families)
- Ensure charts and diagrams use a **unified visual language**
- Templates help enforce unity automatically

---

## 5. Flow

**What it is:** The visual path that directs the reader's eye across the page.

**What it does:** Guides readers to look at content in a deliberate order, ensuring they
see the most important elements first and follow the intended narrative.

**Evaluation question:** *Is there a clear path for the eye to follow, or is it unclear?*

### Best Practices for Slidedocs
- Design pages with a **clear reading order** (typically left-to-right, top-to-bottom)
- Use **visual cues** (arrows, numbering, layout positioning) to guide the eye
- Place the most important element where the eye naturally lands first
- Avoid layouts where the eye bounces randomly between disconnected elements
- Use **alignment** to create invisible lines that guide reading flow
- Section dividers and page transitions should feel natural

---

## How the Five Principles Work Together

```
Contrast  -->  Draws attention to what matters
    |
Whitespace -->  Creates breathing room for focus
    |
Hierarchy -->  Orders information by importance
    |
Unity     -->  Ties everything into a cohesive whole
    |
Flow      -->  Guides the reader through the content
```

When all five principles are applied well, each page passes the **Glance Test**
(see document 04) and the overall document feels professional, clear, and purposeful.
